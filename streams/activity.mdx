the activity stream is one of two inputs to spark. you connect your tools, events flow in, and they naturally group into sessions. one continuous stream — raw events at the bottom, scored work sessions at the top.

## events

every meaningful action across your team's tools becomes an event. a commit. a PR. a deploy. a slack message. an asana task moving to "done."

each event has an **actor** (who), a **source** (where), a **timestamp** (when), and a **payload** (what).

## sessions

events are raw. sessions are meaningful.

spark watches the event stream and groups related events into sessions automatically. a cluster of commits to the same repo by the same person within a time window becomes one session:

<Note>
"dominic worked on the boost split testing feature for 3h20m. 22 commits, 1 PR merged, 3 asana tasks completed."
</Note>

this isn't a separate processing step. it's just what the stream does at higher resolution. the same way you can look at individual waves or see the tide.

## scoring

every session gets evaluated by the **impact scorer** agent. it reads the events, analyzes what changed, and scores the session:

<Tip>scores come with reasoning. the AI explains why it scored the way it did. you can see the logic, disagree, and give feedback that improves future scoring.</Tip>

## sessions for AI agents

<Warning>
when your AI agents work — codex writing code, claude reviewing PRs, custom agents running pipelines — their activity creates sessions too. same stream, same scoring, same visibility.
</Warning>

"what did our agents accomplish today?" is answered the same way as "what did kevin accomplish today?"

## the feed

the activity feed is your view into the stream. a chronological timeline of everything, filterable by actor, source, repo, time, and event type. at event resolution or session resolution. your choice.
