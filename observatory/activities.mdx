activities are the smallest unit in spark. every meaningful event across your team's tools becomes an activity.

## what's an activity?

a commit pushed to github. a PR opened. a deploy triggered. a slack message in your team channel. an asana task moved to "done." a shopify order flagged. a linear issue closed.

each one is a single, timestamped event with an **actor** (who did it), a **source** (where it happened), and a **payload** (what exactly occurred).

activities flow in continuously. you don't create them. they arrive automatically from your connected tools.

## the activity feed

the activity feed is a chronological stream of everything happening across your team. a unified timeline. instead of scrolling through five different tools, everything is in one place.

**filter by:**

| filter | what it does |
|--------|-------------|
| **actor** | everything one person did |
| **source** | everything from one tool (all github activity, all slack activity) |
| **repository/project** | scope to a specific codebase or project |
| **time** | today, this week, custom range |
| **event type** | just commits, just PRs, just deploys |

the feed is useful on its own. it answers "what's happening right now?" at a glance. but its real power is what happens next.

## activities → sessions

raw events are noisy. dozens of commits, PR comments, and slack messages in a day. that's not insight. that's a firehose.

spark watches the activity stream and automatically groups related events into **sessions** — coherent units of work. a cluster of commits to the same repo by the same person within a time window becomes one session:

<Note>
"kevin worked on checkout-v2 for 2h15m, 14 commits, 2 PRs."
</Note>

this is where raw data starts becoming useful. activities are atoms. sessions are the first level of meaning.
